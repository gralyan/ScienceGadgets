Interactive Equation

The interactive equation in Science Gadgets was designed to help students understand the structure of equations and the relationship between their expressions. The traditional method of displaying equations is very minimalistic in its depiction of the natural hierarchical architecture of expressions. Thus, students are required to be very familiar with mathematical conventions and proficient in the ability to mentally parse expressions using mnemonic devices in order to use the equation. The traditional extensive training required to attain such proficiencies requires significant time and focus which naturally deters students from proceeding in mathematically intensive courses and careers. The interactive equation reduces these barriers by providing an intuitive aid for students to explore the relationships between expressions. Built in resources also allow users to learn about algebraic transformations that may be used to simplify and solve the equation. Competences can also be saved, allowing for assessment measurement and progressive rewarding shortcuts which encourage focus on appropriate skills. For more background information, please see the research

Structure

Equations are not linear, they are hierarchical. Yet it's very common for students to attempt "reading" equations from left to right because most languages are read from left to right. The language of math however, requires groupings of functions with varying precedence which may not be immediately obvious with the traditional spatial arrangements of symbols. Problems

One of which common problem involves the use of the order of operations commonly referred to by the mnemonic device PEMDAS. The exponential 2 squared is a group within the product with 3. This product is in another expression, its subtraction from one. To the trained eye, the numbers 1 and 2 are not in the same group (in fast they are two layers away) and therefore cannot interact with one another directly. Their proximity and lack of barriers such as parentheses often leads to mistakes such as subtracting 2 from 1.

Another problem is the group that is negated by the subtraction. The convention that must be used in this case is that exponentials are of higher precedence than a subtraction. The appropriate disambiguation of this expression would be shown as 1-(2^2)*3

The last common misconception in this equation is that the variables labeled b may be cancelled. This would be possible if the numerator was a product of a and b because the division would be equivalent to multiplication with the reciprocal of b and therefore in the same order of precedence as the numerator. Since the numerator is instead a sum, it is a separately grouped layer than the fraction and therefore the b's cannot be cancelled.

Solution

The interactive equation gadget helps clear up these concepts by providing a tangible interface that allows users to visually explore the equation's structure. By clicking into expressions of interest, groupings of contained expressions are exposed. Only one layer is shown at a time which allows students to narrow their focus and reduces overwhelming mental parsing challenges.
